Land in New York is worth a combined $2.8 trillion.

Two thirds of that land is taxed.

The other third, $866 billion, is tax exempt.

The Journal News/lohud.com investigated where that money goes.

Swipe down to find out more.

Hospitals

More than 200 hospitals and health systems in New York receive property tax exemptions.

Hospitals' exempt parcels have risen in value from $13.1 billion to $22.7 billion over the past 16 years.

In 2014 alone, some of these hospitals had $2.6 billion invested in overseas tax havens.

Panama

Hospitals in New York must be nonprofit.

Foreign investments and regulatory avoidance could threaten their tax-exempt status.

Similar behavior by hospitals in New Jersey has prompted lawsuits seeking to remove, or reduce, their tax exemptions.

“If something is getting an exemption, the taxpayers are subsidizing that entity and it has to bear the burden of showing why the exemption is necessary... If you can follow the money into someone’s pocket, that in and of itself may be sufficient to take a (tax exemption) away.”

Martin Allen, attorney for New Jersey communities challenging tax exemptions.

Public Land

New York State owns about 4 million acres of land, including parks and prisons.

The state pays property taxes to counties, schools, and local governments on most of its land.

But not in all counties.

There are about 70,000 acres of state land in Putnam and Rockland counties.

There seems to be little rhyme or reason to where and when the state pays taxes on public land.

New York pays school taxes to Ossining for the Sing Sing prison.

But none to Bedford for the Bedford Hills prison.

New York pays taxes on the Old Croton Aqueduct State Park in Ossining.

But not the Old Croton Aqueduct State Park in Greenburgh, Yonkers, and Mount Pleasant.

Businesses

The $237 million Avalon apartment buildings in New Rochelle were supposed to help revitalize the Sound Shore by attracting millennials.

So the owner received an exemption on its property taxes.

Putting their effective property tax rate at under 1%

The plan to attract millenials didn't pan out.

But the Avalons’ 30-year deals mean the tax rate will stay in place as late as the year 2034.

New York offers thousands of these deals, giving out tax breaks in exchange for job creation.

Statewide, this untaxed land's value has tripled to $34 billion since 1999.

Yet New York's Authorities Budget Office found "little correlation" between these projects and job growth.

"We have seen a massive transfer of public wealth to private hands and the public has gotten almost nothing back"

Richard Brodsky
Senior Fellow, Demos

Universities

Educational institutions are exempt from paying property taxes.

That includes State University of New York (SUNY) campuses, which comprise more than 9,000 acres valued at over $10 billion.

As for private colleges, about $36.5 billion of property is exempt from paying taxes because it's owned by nonprofit educational organizations.

In small municipalities, those missing taxes can have a large impact.

Brockport is a small village in Monroe County.

65% of the village's land is owned by the College at Brockport.

The college doesn't have to pay taxes on the land because it is an educational institution.

But it still benefits from Brockport's services.

Brockport has a $5 million annual budget.

$1.3 million of that goes to its police force.

Police Chief Dan Varrenti argues that this part of the budget wouldn't be needed if not for the college.

Yet the college doesn't pay for the force. Brockport property owners do.

"This has so much to do with our high taxes. If the college were paying into our tax levy, our rate of $11.87 would go down to $4.30"

    Margary Blackman
     Brockport mayor

Religious Organizations

New York is one of the least religious states in the country, with just 29% of people attending weekly worship services.

Yet the state gives out millions in property tax exemptions to religious groups. This exempt land's value has risen to nearly $26 billion in 2015.

This rise in exemptions has been dramatic in Rockland County's Ramapo, which has a booming population of synagogues and yeshivas.

Ramapo went from having 150 exempt properties in 1999 to 523 in 2015.

There are now more clergy residences in Ramapo, population 127 thousand, than anywhere in the state outside of New York City, population 8.6 million.
